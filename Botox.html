Botulinum toxin (Botox)


Botox is an injectable medication commonly used in beauty care. It works on the muscles by reducing their contraction. As such, in neurological diseases characterised by muscle stiffness, botox may help in selected cases.

As movement disorders specialists, we use botox primarily in dystonias which are characterised by excessive muscle contraction. Common indications include blepharospasm (eyes closing involuntarily due to eyelid spasm), hemifacial spasm (twitching of one side of the face), oromandibular and lingual dystonia (abnormal mouth, tongue and jaw movements), cervical dystonias (abnormal neck posturing), writers cramp (abnormal contraction of the upper limb muscles while writing) and focal limb dystonias.

Apart from these, botox can also help in patients with palmoplantar hyperhydrosis (hand and foot increased sweating), sialorrhea (increased salivation and drooling), post stroke limb stiffness, and certain painful conditions such as trigeminal neuralgia (a specific type of facial pain).

Botox is a highly effective first line medication in many of the above indications. However, it acts for around 3 to 6 months, after which patients need a repeat dose depending on the symptom. Inspite of this, a number of patients opt for the treatment in view of the improvement in their quality of life.
